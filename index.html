🟠

Morphine

Pain, breathlessness

Core kit ▾

What it is for

  • Relieving pain that other medicines are not controlling
  • Easing the distressing sensation of breathlessness or air hunger
  • Does not treat the underlying illness — makes the person more comfortable

Usual dosing (under the skin)

Typical when-needed dose

2.5–5 mg under the skin

Onset

20–30 minutes

Minimum interval

4 hours (unless nurse advises otherwise)

Duration of effect

4–6 hours

Signs it may be needed

  • Facial grimacing, wincing, or moaning
  • Restlessness not helped by repositioning or reassurance
  • Breathing looks laboured and is visibly distressing to them

"Will morphine make them die sooner?"
At doses prescribed for comfort, morphine does not hasten death. Research supports this. It is given to reduce suffering, not to shorten life.

⚠️ Do not repeat a dose sooner than your nurse advised. Record every dose given.

🟤

Fentanyl (patch)

Continuous pain control

Extended kit ▾

What it is for

  • Providing continuous background pain relief over 72 hours
  • Used when morphine cannot be tolerated or swallowed medicines are no longer possible
  • Delivered through a patch applied to the skin

How to use the patch

  • Apply to flat, hairless skin on the upper arm, chest, or back — not over bony areas
  • Change every 72 hours (3 days) — write the date and time on the patch in texta
  • Rotate sites to avoid skin irritation
  • Old patches still contain fentanyl — fold sticky sides together and return to pharmacy

Dosing

Onset (new patch)

12–24 hours to full effect

Patch change

Every 72 hours

Important: Fentanyl patches provide steady background pain relief only — you may still need extra morphine when needed for sudden pain flares. Do not cut, heat, or expose the patch to direct sunlight.

🔵

Midazolam

Anxiety, agitation, seizures

Core kit ▾

What it is for

  • Severe anxiety or panic that cannot be settled by reassurance alone
  • Distressing agitation and restlessness
  • Stopping or reducing seizures if they occur

Usual dosing (under the skin)

Typical when-needed dose

2.5–5 mg under the skin

Onset

15–20 minutes

Minimum interval

2–4 hours

Duration

2–4 hours

Signs it may be needed

  • Picking at bedclothes, pulling at clothing or tubes
  • Crying out or calling repeatedly despite reassurance
  • Muscle jerking or seizure occurring

First time using for agitation — call your nurse first. They can confirm it is appropriate for what you are seeing and walk you through it over the phone.

🟡

Clonazepam

Anxiety, muscle jerks, seizures

Extended kit ▾

What it is for

  • Sustained anxiety or agitation, particularly when longer duration is needed than midazolam provides
  • Sudden muscle jerks that can occur near end of life
  • Seizure management or prevention

How it is given

Can be given under the tongue (as a wafer or drops) or under the skin. Under-the-tongue is useful when an injection is difficult. Onset is 20–30 minutes; duration up to 6–8 hours.

Note: Clonazepam and midazolam both calm the nervous system in a similar way. Your nurse or doctor will specify which one to use and when — do not use both without instruction.

🟣

Haloperidol

Confusion, nausea

Core kit ▾

What it is for

  • Reducing sudden confusion when causing visible distress
  • Helping with distressing hallucinations
  • Controlling nausea and vomiting when other measures are insufficient

Usual dosing

Typical when-needed dose

0.5–1.5 mg under the skin

Onset

20–30 minutes

Not all confusion needs treatment. Near end of life, some visions and confusion can be peaceful for the person even if difficult to witness. Haloperidol is most appropriate when the confusion is clearly causing the person distress.

🩷

Metoclopramide

Nausea, vomiting, slow stomach emptying

Extended kit ▾

What it is for

  • Nausea and vomiting, particularly when related to the stomach not emptying properly
  • Useful when nausea is brought on by movement or eating
  • Can be given under the skin or via a small continuous pump under the skin

How it works

Metoclopramide speeds up gastric emptying and helps the stomach empty and reduces sickness signals in the brain. It is particularly useful when nausea is linked to strong pain medicines or a slow bowel.

⚠️ Avoid in bowel obstruction. Do not use if haloperidol is also being given for nausea without guidance — they work in a similar way and using both together increases the risk of side effects.

🟢

Hyoscine butylbromide

Noisy breathing, secretions

Core kit ▾

What it is for

  • Reducing the gurgling or rattling sound in breathing near end of life
  • This occurs because the person can no longer swallow their own secretions — it is not choking

Before giving it

Try gently repositioning onto their side first. This alone often reduces the sound and should always be attempted before giving the medicine.

Dosing

Typical dose

20 mg under the skin

Onset

20–30 minutes

The person is not aware of or distressed by this sound. Research consistently shows this. It is appropriate to give this medicine as much for your own comfort as for theirs. You do not need to justify it.

🔷

Glycopyrronium

Alternative for secretions

Extended kit ▾

What it is for

  • An alternative to hyoscine butylbromide for managing fluid in the airways that causes noisy breathing
  • Preferred in some patients because — unlike hyoscine — it does not cross into the brain, so it causes less confusion and extra sleepiness

How it differs from hyoscine

  • Does not cause drowsiness or confusion (hyoscine can)
  • Used when the person is still somewhat alert and extra sleepiness would be unwanted
  • Both work equally well for reducing secretion noises

Your kit will contain either hyoscine butylbromide or glycopyrronium — rarely both. Check your medicines with your nurse so you know which one you have.

🌟

Dexamethasone

Inflammation, appetite, nausea

Extended kit ▾

What it is for

  • Reducing inflammation — particularly useful for brain tumour swelling, spinal cord compression, or bowel obstruction
  • Improving appetite and general sense of wellbeing in the short term
  • Helping with nausea, particularly when caused by pressure inside the head
  • Reducing breathlessness caused by inflammation around tumours

How it is given

Can be given by mouth (as a tablet), under the skin, or via a small continuous pump under the skin. Usually given in the morning to avoid disrupting sleep. Your nurse will specify the dose and timing.

⚠️ Dexamethasone should not be stopped suddenly — it needs to be tapered off. Do not adjust the dose without your nurse or doctor's advice. It can cause mood changes, increased appetite, and blood sugar rises even in the short term.

Signs that death may be very close

In the final hours: breathing becomes very irregular with long pauses, a mottled or blotchy pattern appears on legs and feet, hands and feet turn cool while the face remains warm, the person becomes completely unresponsive. These are normal and expected. You do not need to do anything medical. Contact your nurse — they can come to be with you, or stay on the phone and guide you through what is happening.

Injection under the skin — step by step

1

Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water for at least 20 seconds.

2

Check: right medicine, right dose, right person. Check the expiry date on the vial.

3

Draw up the prescribed dose from the vial using the syringe as your nurse showed you.

4

Clean the injection site (upper outer arm or abdomen) with an alcohol swab. Let it dry completely — do not inject onto a wet swab.

5

Gently pinch the skin. Insert the needle at a 45° angle smoothly and steadily. Inject slowly.

6

Remove the needle. Apply gentle pressure with a cotton ball. Do not rub.

7

Record immediately: time given, which medicine, dose, reason, your initials. Your nurse needs this at every visit.

Record every dose — what to write

⏰ Time given 💊 Medicine name 📏 Dose (mg) 📝 Reason ✍️ Your initials

Your nurse advises a minimum time between doses. Do not give another dose earlier without calling first — they can authorise it over the phone if needed.

Rotating injection sites

↻

Alternate sites with each injection — upper outer arm (left/right), abdomen (left/right of navel). Avoid bruised, red, or broken skin. Your nurse can draw a simple rotation guide if helpful.

Safe storage and disposal

These are controlled medicines. Keep locked away from children and visitors at all times. After the person has died, return any unused medicines to a pharmacy — do not put them in household rubbish, flush them, or give them to anyone else.

Syringe drivers: Some patients are prescribed a syringe driver — a small pump that delivers a continuous dose of medicines under the skin over 24 hours. If one has been set up, your nurse will visit daily to check and refill it. Do not adjust the rate or touch the driver. Call your nurse if the alarm sounds or the driver stops.

Looking after yourself

Caring for someone at end of life is one of the most demanding things a person can do. Your wellbeing matters.

✅ Carer readiness checklist

0 of 8 complete

🧠 It is okay to feel overwhelmed

Fear, grief, exhaustion, and doubt are all normal. Feeling frightened before giving a medicine, or uncertain whether you are doing the right thing, does not mean you are failing. It means you are paying attention — and that is exactly what the person you love needs.

🛌 Sleep when you can

Caring through the night is exhausting. Ask family or friends to take shifts. Some palliative care services can arrange overnight volunteer sitters — ask your nurse whether this is available in your area.

📞 Support contacts

  • Your palliative care nurse — they expect your calls day and night. You are not bothering them.
  • Griefline: 1300 845 745 — anticipatory grief support, evenings and weekends
  • Carer Gateway: 1800 422 737 — practical support, emergency respite, carer navigation
  • Healthdirect: 1800 022 222 — free 24/7 nurse advice

🕊 After the death

There is no immediate rush. Take time to sit with them. When ready, call your palliative care nurse or GP — not 000 — unless there is something unexpected. Calling 000 for an expected death can trigger processes the person would not have wanted.

Get help now

If you are unsure — call. You are not expected to handle this alone.

Before calling 000 — important

Calling 000 for a palliative care patient at home can trigger resuscitation attempts — even if the person has clearly said they do not want this. Call your palliative care nurse or GP first in almost every situation. Call 000 only for emergencies genuinely outside the expected course of illness, or if anyone is in immediate danger.
